Can dogs detect cancer? A hypothesis testing case.

Thu 06 May 2021
By Sobir Bobiev
Category: probability and statistics

The following problem was presented in this lecture.

Cancer detecting dogs. Dogs were trained to detect bladder cancer according to the scent of a patients urine. After training the dogs were tested on seven urine samples, where only one was from a cancer patient. The experiment was repeated on 89 different dogs. The dogs correctly identified the cancer 35 times.

Now, the question is, are dogs doing better than random?

What is random? If dogs decided randomly, then the probability of detecting the cancer would be \(p=1/7\) and the expected number of correct detection would be \(89 \cdot \frac{1}{7} = 12.714\). In our case this number, which is \(35\), seems to be a little high. Randomness exists, sure, but how much? Depends on the standard deviation.

Ok, let's state the hypotheses now:

$$ \begin{align} H_0 &: p = 1/7 \\ H_1 &: p > 1/7 \end{align} $$

Assuming the null hypotheses, the distribution of number of times dogs correctly detect cancer is binomial \(B(89, 1/7)\).

Let's plot this and see where our sample lies:

Yes, the probability of getting 35 or more correct detections under the null hypothesis is almost zero! Dogs are great at sniffing!

Note that binomial distribution for large enough values of \(n\) (\(>20\)) is very close to normal distribution. In other words, we could use normal distribution instead.
