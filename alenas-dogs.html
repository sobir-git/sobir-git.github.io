Alena's dogs

Thu 06 May 2021
By Sobir Bobiev
Category: probability and statistics

The following is a discussion of a problem in hypothesis testing, presented in Khan Academy here.

The problem description starts like this:

Alena has a pair of dogs, and she noticed they usually breed more male puppies than females. To check that, she bred them again and obtained 15 puppies, and 13 of them were males.

Now, is that a coincidence that her dogs breed more males than females?

Let's assume the null hypothesis: \(50\%\) of the time her dogs breed males. Alternative hypothesis: more than \(50\%\) of the time her dogs breed males.

More formally, let \(p\) be the true probability of her dogs breeding male. Then

$$ \begin{align} H_0 &: p = 0.5 \\ H_1 &: p > 0.5 \end{align} $$

When \(H_0\) assumed, the distribution of the bred male percentage follows the binomial distribution \(B(15, 0.5)\).

In fact, as you can see below \(P(X \geq 13) = 0.0037\) which is very low. This is not a coincidence at all!
